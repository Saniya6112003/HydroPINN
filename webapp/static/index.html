🌎 How routes are colour-coded by safety

  • Each road segment's GPS midpoint is mapped to the nearest grid cell in the PINN flood prediction.

📊 What is MC Dropout uncertainty?

The model's Dropout layers (which randomly silence 10% of neurons during training) are kept active during inference. Running 50 prediction passes gives 50 slightly different flood maps. The standard deviation across those 50 maps = model uncertainty. Bright regions on the uncertainty map = areas where the model is less confident — emergency responders should prioritise sensor deployment there.

📚 Explainer

How HydroPINN Works

End-to-end pipeline from raw satellite data to real-world actionable flood intelligence.

1

🌏 Terrain Data — NASA SRTM Satellite DEM

A 64×64 grid of Mumbai's elevation (2.9m–15m) from NASA's Shuttle Radar Topography Mission. Colaba sits high in the south; Santacruz and Dharavi are low-lying in the north. Terrain slope tells the PINN which way water flows — it's computed as dz/dx and dz/dy via finite differences and passed as fixed inputs.

2

🌧 Rainfall Model — Gaussian Storm Field

The 2005 storm is modelled as a Gaussian intensity field peaking over Santacruz at hour 8 with 944mm/24hr at centre. Rainfall R(x,y,t) is an input to the PINN at both training collocation points and inference time — so the model knows where and when rain is falling.

3

🧠 PINN Training — Data Loss + Physics Loss

The neural network (6 layers × 128 neurons, tanh, Dropout 10%) is trained with three simultaneous losses: L_data — match 200 sparse sensor readings; L_pde — satisfy the Shallow Water Equations at 10,000 random points; L_bc — zero depth at domain boundaries. Curriculum learning: 400 epoch data-only warmup, then 1,600 epochs combined. L-BFGS fine-tunes for 300 steps.

4

🔮 Flood Prediction — 20 Timesteps × 64×64 Grid

The trained model evaluates h(x,y,t), u(x,y,t), v(x,y,t) at every cell of the 64×64 grid for 20 equally-spaced times (0–24 hours). That's 81,920 depth predictions forming a complete 4D flood simulation. The velocity fields u and v show flow direction — water funnelling from north to south along Mumbai's natural drainage.

5

🚨 Emergency Response — Risk + Routes + Uncertainty

Peak depths are classified into 4 risk levels. Dijkstra's algorithm on a flood-penalised graph finds minimum-risk paths. MC Dropout quantifies model confidence. Grid coordinates are mapped to real Mumbai GPS (lat 18.91°N–19.28°N), OSRM fetches real road routes, and each segment is coloured by the flood depth at that precise location on the ground.

⚡ The core innovation in one sentence

Standard ML: "What pattern fits the data?"  |  PINN: "What pattern fits the data AND obeys the laws of fluid physics?"

With only 200 sensors across all of Mumbai, a plain NN has no guidance between sensors. The PINN is guided by physical laws everywhere — even in places no sensor has ever been. That makes it safe to deploy in real emergency systems.

🔬 Model Internals

Model Deep Dive

Architecture, training dynamics, physics violations, flow fields, weight distributions — everything under the hood.

🧠 — parameters
📈 6 hidden layers × 128 neurons
🔬 tanh activation → softplus output
🆕 Dropout 10% (also used for MC uncertainty)
🆕 Adam + L-BFGS two-phase training
💥
Why tanh?

The physics loss requires computing second-order derivatives of the network output via autograd. tanh is smooth and infinitely differentiable — its gradients never vanish to exactly zero. ReLU breaks at the kink and its 2nd derivative is zero everywhere, making the SWE residuals uncomputable.

🔒
Why softplus for h?

softplus(x) = ln(1 + eˣ) is always positive. Water depth can never be negative. Using softplus on the h output head enforces this physical constraint architecturally — the network literally cannot predict h < 0, unlike a plain linear head.

🎲
Why Dropout at inference?

Normally Dropout is disabled during inference (eval mode). HydroPINN keeps it ON during inference for 50 passes — called MC Dropout. The variance across passes = prediction uncertainty. This is Bayesian deep learning without the computational cost of a full Bayesian network.

🚀
Two-phase optimisation

Phase 1: Adam (stochastic gradient, lr=1e-3) explores broadly for 2000 epochs — fast but noisy. Phase 2: L-BFGS (quasi-Newton, second-order method) converges sharply in 300 steps. This mimics how researchers converge PINN solutions in literature.

🏴
Curriculum learning

First 400 epochs: data loss only (lambda_pde=0). This prevents the physics loss from "overwhelming" gradient signals before the network has learned the basic depth pattern. The physics is then added gradually — the same curriculum approach used in PINN research for complex equations.

🔌
Input normalisation

z ∈ [2.9, 15m] and R ∈ [0, 100 mm/hr] are normalised to [0,1] inside the forward pass. Without this, tanh(15) ≈ tanh(3) ≈ 1.0 — the network becomes blind to terrain differences. This was the root cause of the near-zero depth predictions in early training runs.

⚙ Loss function: three competing objectives

L = 5.0 × L_data + 0.0001 × L_pde + 0.005 × L_bc

L_data — MSE between predicted and observed h at 200 sparse sensor points. Weight 5.0 — the dominant signal.
L_pde — Mean squared SWE residuals at 10,000 random collocation points throughout the domain. Weight 0.0001 — acts as a weak physics regulariser. (Larger weights cause gradient conflicts in normalised coordinates.)
L_bc — h forced to 0 at domain boundary cells. Weight 0.005 — prevents unrealistic edge effects.

⚡ What these residuals tell us

Continuity residual (mass conservation): Where the model violates "water in = water out + rain". High values near Dharavi and the Mithi River show areas where the simplified SWE struggles with the complex topography of river confluence zones.

Momentum residuals: Large values indicate where inertial forces (acceleration terms) conflict with pressure and terrain slope. In urban flooding, the dominant physics is actually quasi-static (slow flow), so large momentum residuals are expected — the momentum equations are less critical than continuity.

Why residuals look large in normalised coordinates: The SWE is written in physical units (m, s, m/s) but the network inputs t and x are normalised to [0,1]. This creates scale factors of ~86400 (seconds/day) and ~30000 (domain width in metres) that inflate all derivative terms. The residuals are physically modest despite the large normalised values.

💡 Why z and R sensitivity may be near-zero

If ∂h/∂z and ∂h/∂R are near zero, it means the model converged to a solution driven primarily by the spatial-temporal pattern (x, y, t) rather than terrain and rainfall. This is a known challenge in PINN training: the data loss gradient can dominate and the network learns a "shortcut" based on location alone. The spatial gradients ∂h/∂x and ∂h/∂y being non-zero confirm the model is still geographically aware — it just isn't using terrain and rainfall as differentiating factors. Solution: longer training with higher lambda_pde after the curriculum warmup.

🎲 Weight Distributions per Layer

📈 What healthy weights look like

Xavier uniform initialisation starts weights in ~[−0.22, +0.22] for the first layer. After training, weights spread out into a roughly Gaussian distribution. Extremely large weights (>2) indicate the model is "overconfident" about specific features. Very narrow distributions (<0.1 std) suggest the layer is underutilised.

💧 tanh Activation Saturation per Layer

⚡ What saturation means

tanh saturates at ±1 — neurons stuck there produce zero gradient (the "vanishing gradient" problem). A saturation % above 30% means many neurons aren't contributing useful signals. The histogram shape shows whether activations are spreading across the full [-1, 1] range (healthy) or piling up at the edges (saturated).
